Fission was discovered in 1938 by the German physicist Otto Hahn, and the theory behind it was worked out by Lise Meitner the next year. However, it would take 6 more years before it could be harnessed for use in a bomb.



A-Bombs

A-Bombs work by causing a nuclear fission chain reaction. In the center of every atom, there is an object called a nucleus, which is composed of two types of particles: protons, and neutrons. In some cases, if the number of protons and neutrons is not correctly balanced, the nucleus may become unstable. If an atom in this situation is hit by a stray neutron, it can split, releasing energy and a few more neutrons. H-Bombs

An H-Bomb, or "Thermonuclear Bomb" is a more powerful atomic weapon, based on fusion, instead of fission. It has two parts: A Primary, which is itself an A-bomb, and a Secondary, which contains light elements (that is, elements with small nuclei). When the Primary explodes, it produces a huge amount of energy and pressure, which causes some of the light nuclei in the Secondary to fuse together, releasing a large amount of energy in the process.
